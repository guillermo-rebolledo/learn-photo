## ISO changes the rendering, not the light gathered

In a digital Camera, aperture and shutter speed govern **Captured Light**. ISO changes how the captured signal is rendered: raising ISO increases **Rendered Brightness**, but also makes noise and reduced fine detail more visible. ISO does not gather more light.

You may hear aperture, shutter speed, and ISO grouped as the **Exposure Triangle**. That is useful outside shorthand, but the three controls are not physically equivalent. In this Digital-First Model, choose aperture and shutter for light, depth, and motion; then choose an ISO compatible with the brightness and image-quality tradeoff you can accept.

The low-light preview is a calibrated Conceptual Simulator, not a prediction for every Camera. Sensor design, processing, temperature, and underexposure all affect real noise.

## Film speed is chosen for the roll

Digital ISO can change for every photograph. Film speed describes the sensitivity of the film already loaded, so a Film Constraint fixes ISO across the roll and leaves aperture and shutter speed to solve the photograph.

A slower film such as ISO 100 generally suits abundant light and can offer finer grain. A faster film such as ISO 400 or 800 is more practical in lower light or when faster shutters are needed, usually with more visible grain. Grain is the film image’s visible texture; it is not digital noise, and its appearance also depends on film format, enlargement, and the chosen stock.

Choose a practical speed for the light and the apertures or shutters you expect to need. This Lesson keeps the comparison intentionally narrow and does not model advanced processing or individual stock rendering.
